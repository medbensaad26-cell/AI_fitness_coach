# Rotator Cuff Health and Overhead Pressing Safety

## Anatomy & Function
The rotator cuff comprises the supraspinatus, infraspinatus, teres minor, and subscapularis. These muscles stabilize the humeral head within the shallow glenoid fossa, enabling fine control of arm rotation and elevation. The supraspinatus is most vulnerable because it passes through the narrow subacromial space.

## Pathomechanics of Impingement
Overhead movements with the humerus in internal rotation and abduction (e.g., behind-the-neck press, upright row with high elbows) compress the supraspinatus tendon and subacromial bursa against the acromion. Chronic impingement leads to tendinopathy, bursitis, and eventually rotator cuff tears.

## Safe Pressing Guidelines
- **Elbow position**: Maintain elbows at a 30–45° angle from the torso during overhead presses (scapular plane). This opens the subacromial space.
- **Grip width**: A grip just outside shoulder-width minimizes stress.
- **Alternative exercises**: Landmine press, dumbbell neutral-grip press, and push‑ups with elbows tucked are safer for individuals with cuff sensitivity.
- **Prehabilitation**: Strengthen external rotators (e.g., side-lying external rotation, band pull-aparts) and scapular stabilizers (serratus anterior, lower trapezius) to reduce injury risk.

## Red Flags & Modifications
- Sharp anterior shoulder pain during pressing → stop, reduce load, switch to neutral grip.
- Pain radiating down the arm → may indicate cervical spine involvement; refer to a clinician.
- No behind-the-neck pressing, ever. It places the shoulder in maximum external rotation and abduction, the most vulnerable position.

## Evidence Notes
Multiple systematic reviews confirm that scapular-plane elevation and external rotation strengthening reduce subacromial impingement symptoms (Kibler et al., 2013; Escamilla et al., 2014).
