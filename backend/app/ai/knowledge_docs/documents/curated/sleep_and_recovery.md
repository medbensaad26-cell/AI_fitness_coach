# Sleep: The Foundation of Recovery and Performance

## Physiology of Sleep & Muscle Repair
During deep (slow‑wave) sleep, the pituitary gland pulses growth hormone (GH), which stimulates muscle protein synthesis, collagen repair, and bone remodeling. REM sleep consolidates motor skills, critical for athletic learning. Cortisol, a catabolic hormone, is suppressed during early sleep but rises naturally toward morning.

## Recommended Sleep Durations
- **Adults (18–64)**: 7–9 hours per night.
- **Athletes/Heavy Trainers**: 8–10 hours, especially during high‑volume blocks.
- **Adolescent athletes**: 9–10 hours due to developmental demands.

## Consequences of Sleep Deficiency
- ↓ Muscle protein synthesis, ↑ cortisol → impaired hypertrophy.
- Reduced reaction time, accuracy, and decision‑making.
- Increased injury risk (studies show sleep <6h doubles injury risk in youth athletes).
- Altered glucose metabolism and appetite dysregulation (ghrelin/leptin imbalance).

## Practical Sleep Hygiene for Athletes
- **Consistency**: Same bed/wake time, even on weekends.
- **Environment**: Cool (65–68°F / 18–20°C), dark, quiet.
- **Screen curfew**: No screens 60–90 min before bed; blue light suppresses melatonin.
- **Pre‑sleep routine**: Light stretching, reading (non‑stimulating), deep breathing.
- **Nutrition**: Avoid large meals, caffeine (after 2 PM), and alcohol near bedtime.
- **Naps**: 20–30 min power naps early afternoon can supplement, not replace, night sleep.

## Special Considerations
- **Travel/jet lag**: Adjust sleep schedule gradually; use melatonin (3–5 mg) for short‑term jet lag.
- **Shift workers**: Prioritize a consistent sleep block even if timing is odd; use blackout curtains.

## Role of the Coach
Monitor fatigue markers (mood, motivation, soreness). If a client consistently sleeps <7h, prioritize sleep improvement over training intensity. Recovery is training.
