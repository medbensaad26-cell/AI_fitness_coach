# Maintaining a Neutral Spine During Resistance Training

## Definition & Biomechanical Rationale
A neutral spine preserves the natural lordotic curve of the lumbar region (~40–50°), thoracic kyphosis, and cervical lordosis. This alignment distributes compressive forces evenly across intervertebral discs and facet joints. Flexion (rounding) under load displaces the nucleus pulposus posteriorly, increasing disc herniation risk. Hyperextension compresses facet joints and pars interarticularis.

## Bracing: The Core's Protective Mechanism
- **Technique**: Inhale deeply, expand the abdomen 360°, and tighten as if expecting a punch. This activates the transversus abdominis, internal/external obliques, and pelvic floor, creating a rigid cylinder.
- **Valsalva maneuver**: Used naturally during heavy lifts; it elevates intra‑abdominal pressure but transiently increases blood pressure. Contraindicated for individuals with uncontrolled hypertension.
- **Belt use**: A belt provides external feedback for bracing but does not replace proper core activation.

## Lift-Specific Spine Safety
- **Deadlift**: Hinge at hips, maintain a flat back, "pull the slack out of the bar" before initiating. Avoid jerking.
- **Squat**: Brace before descending; avoid excessive anterior pelvic tilt ("butt wink") by not going deeper than your mobility allows.
- **Row/Good Morning**: Keep the back rigid; flex at the hips, not the waist.
- **Overhead press**: Tight glutes and core prevent lumbar hyperextension.

## Regressions for At-Risk Individuals
- Replace loaded spinal flexion (crunches, sit‑ups) with anti‑extension movements (plank, dead bug, Pallof press).
- Substitute barbell back squats with goblet squats or belt squats if lumbar control is poor.
- Use trap bar deadlifts instead of conventional deadlifts to reduce spinal shear.

## Coaching Cue Summary
- "Brace for a punch."
- "Chest up, ribs down."
- "Hinge at the hips, not the waist."
- "Control the weight; never collapse."

## Scientific Consensus
Decades of in vivo and cadaveric research (McGill, 2015; Adams & Dolan, 2005) demonstrate that maintaining a neutral spine significantly reduces disc injury risk under compressive load.
