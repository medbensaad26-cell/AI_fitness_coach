# Hydration Strategies for Optimal Exercise Performance

## Physiological Importance
Water constitutes ~60% of body mass and is essential for thermoregulation, cardiovascular function, nutrient transport, and joint lubrication. Even mild dehydration (2% body mass loss) impairs aerobic endurance, cognitive function, and strength endurance.

## Pre‑Exercise Hydration
- Begin exercise euhydrated. Urine color should be pale straw.
- 4 hours prior: drink 5–7 mL/kg body weight slowly.
- 2 hours prior if urine is dark or no urine: drink an additional 3–5 mL/kg.
- Hyperhydration with glycerol is not recommended for general athletes.

## During Exercise
- Goal: Prevent water loss >2% body mass.
- General guideline: 0.4–0.8 L/h, adjusted for sweat rate, sport, and environment.
- Sweat rate calculation: (pre‑exercise weight – post‑exercise weight + fluid consumed – urine output) / exercise time.
- For exercise >60–90 min or in heat: use a beverage with 6–8% carbohydrate and sodium (0.5–0.7 g/L) to enhance fluid absorption and maintain blood glucose.

## Post‑Exercise Rehydration
- Replace 1.25–1.5 L of fluid per kg body weight lost.
- Include sodium (e.g., food, electrolyte drink) to retain ingested fluid and stimulate thirst.
- Avoid overconsumption of plain water without electrolytes to prevent exercise‑associated hyponatremia.

## Electrolyte Essentials
- **Sodium**: Primary electrolyte lost in sweat; replacement critical for fluid balance and nerve function.
- **Potassium**: Minor losses; easily replenished through fruits and vegetables.
- **Magnesium**: Involved in muscle contraction; deficiency may exacerbate cramping.

## Special Populations & Conditions
- **Hot/humid environments**: Increase fluid intake, add electrolytes, acclimatize over 10–14 days.
- **Cold environments**: Thirst is blunted; schedule drinking.
- **Older adults**: Reduced thirst sensation; proactive hydration scheduling is key.

## Coaching Cues
- "Drink to thirst during short/easy sessions; for longer/harder efforts, plan intake."
- "Weigh yourself before and after a few training sessions to learn your sweat rate."
- "Clear urine = overhydrated; dark = dehydrated; pale yellow = just right."
