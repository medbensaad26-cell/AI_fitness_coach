# Sleep and Athletic Performance: Key Findings

## Sleep Architecture & Recovery
- Growth hormone (GH) is primarily secreted during slow‑wave sleep (SWS); it facilitates protein synthesis, bone repair, and immune function.
- Cortisol, a catabolic hormone, is lowest during early sleep and rises in the early morning. Inadequate sleep can shift this balance, increasing catabolism.

## Performance Effects of Sleep Loss
- **Cognitive**: Reduced reaction time, decision‑making, focus; critical for tactical athletes and skill execution.
- **Physical**: Decreased maximal strength, sprint times, and endurance performance are observed with sleep restriction (≤6 h/night for multiple nights).
- **Injury risk**: Adolescent athletes sleeping <8 h/night were 1.7× more likely to have a sports injury (Milewski et al., 2014).

## Recommendations for Athletes
- **Target**: 7–9 h for adults, 9–10 h for adolescents; extend to 10 h during heavy training or competition.
- **Sleep hygiene**: Maintain a consistent schedule, cool/dark room, limit blue light exposure 1–2 h before bed.
- **Napping**: 20–30 min power naps can supplement but not replace night sleep.
- **Travel**: Plan for jet lag; allow 1 day per time zone crossed for full adaptation.

## Practical Coaching Implications
Coaches should integrate sleep screening into athlete monitoring. A simple question like “How many hours of sleep did you get last night?” can predict training readiness and injury risk.
