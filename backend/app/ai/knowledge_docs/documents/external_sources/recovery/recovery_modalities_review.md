# Evidence‑Based Recovery Techniques: Foam Rolling, Active Recovery, and More

## Methodological Overview
A 2018 systematic review and meta‑analysis examined various post‑exercise recovery strategies for reducing muscle damage markers (creatine kinase), delayed onset muscle soreness (DOMS), and inflammation. The most effective, low‑cost, and practical modalities are summarized here.

## Foam Rolling (Self‑Myofascial Release)
- **Effect on DOMS**: Moderate to large effect in reducing perceived pain at 24–72 h post‑exercise.
- **Mechanism**: Likely involves mechanoreceptor stimulation and reduced tissue adhesion, rather than actual “breaking up” of fascia.
- **Protocol**: Roll each major muscle group for 1–2 min at a slow, controlled pace, focusing on tender spots. Perform immediately post‑exercise or later in the day.
- **Performance**: Does not impair subsequent performance; may actually improve range of motion and reduce muscle stiffness.

## Active Recovery
- **Definition**: Low‑intensity aerobic exercise (e.g., cycling at 30–40% VO₂max, light swimming) performed after strenuous exercise.
- **Benefits**: Accelerates blood lactate clearance, reduces muscle soreness, and attenuates inflammation compared to passive rest.
- **Prescription**: 15–20 minutes immediately after training or competition.

## Other Modalities – Brief Notes
- **Cold water immersion (CWI)**: Effective for reducing DOMS and inflammation; 10–15°C for 10–15 minutes. May blunt long‑term strength adaptations if used chronically after every session.
- **Compression garments**: Provide a small, inconsistent effect on recovery; may be placebo or related to increased blood flow.
- **Stretching**: Static stretching post‑exercise does not reduce muscle soreness; dynamic mobility work is preferred.

## Practical Takeaway for Coaches
Prioritize **foam rolling** and **active recovery** as immediate, zero‑cost interventions that can be done in any gym. Reserve CWI for competition periods. Educate clients that some soreness is normal and a sign of adaptation, not a failure of recovery.
