# NSCA Guidelines for Resistance Training Progression

## The Principle of Progressive Overload
For continued adaptation, the training stimulus must be increased over time. This can be achieved by manipulating load, volume, frequency, or exercise complexity.

## Progression for Beginners
- **Load**: Start with light loads (40–60% 1RM) to master technique. Increase by 5–10% when the target repetitions can be completed comfortably.
- **Volume**: 1–3 sets of 8–12 reps per exercise.
- **Frequency**: 2–3 sessions per week, whole‑body or split routine.
- **Progression**: After 2–4 weeks of consistent training, increase load first, then sets. Introduce new exercises gradually.

## Intermediate Progression
- **Load**: Cycle between moderate (60–75% 1RM) and heavy (80–90% 1RM) days.
- **Volume**: 3–5 sets of 6–12 reps, with periodized variation (hypertrophy, strength, power phases).
- **Techniques**: Incorporate supersets, drop sets, and tempo changes for novel stimulus.
- **Progression**: Linear periodization (increasing intensity, decreasing volume) or undulating periodization (daily/weekly variations) are both effective.

## Advanced Progression
- Use advanced methods: accommodating resistance (bands/chains), eccentric overload, cluster sets.
- Program near‑maximal loads (≥90% 1RM) strategically, with adequate recovery.
- Individualize based on competition schedule or specific goals.

## Progression for Special Populations
- **Older adults**: Emphasize power (lighter loads moved quickly) to improve functional capacity; progress volume before intensity.
- **Injury rehabilitation**: Progress from isometric → dynamic, machine → free weight, stable → unstable surfaces, based on tissue healing timelines.
- **Youth**: Focus on technique and motor control; load progression is secondary to movement quality.

## Monitoring & Adjusting
Use training logs to track load, reps, and perceived exertion. If performance stalls for 2–3 sessions, evaluate recovery, nutrition, or sleep before increasing training stress. Deload every 4–8 weeks to prevent overtraining.
