# The Vertebral Column and Spinal Stability

## Structure of the Spine
The vertebral column consists of 24 movable vertebrae (7 cervical, 12 thoracic, 5 lumbar) plus the sacrum and coccyx. Between adjacent vertebral bodies lie intervertebral discs—fibrocartilage pads that absorb shock and permit motion. Each disc has a tough outer annulus fibrosus and a gel‑like nucleus pulposus.

## Natural Curvatures
- **Cervical lordosis** (anterior convexity)
- **Thoracic kyphosis** (posterior convexity)
- **Lumbar lordosis** (anterior convexity)

These curves distribute mechanical load efficiently. Loss of curvature (e.g., flat back) or excessive curvature (hyperlordosis/hyperkyphosis) can predispose to disc degeneration and muscle imbalance.

## Ligamentous Support
- **Anterior longitudinal ligament** – resists hyperextension.
- **Posterior longitudinal ligament** – resists hyperflexion, located inside the vertebral canal.
- **Ligamentum flavum** – connects laminae, maintains upright posture.

## Core Musculature & Stability
The “core” includes not just the rectus abdominis but:
- **Transversus abdominis** – deepest layer, compresses abdomen, increases intra‑abdominal pressure.
- **Multifidus** – short, deep muscles between vertebrae; crucial for segmental stability.
- **Pelvic floor and diaphragm** – top and bottom of the cylinder.

Co‑contraction of these muscles creates a rigid cylinder, stiffening the spine against buckling under load. This is the biomechanical basis of bracing during lifting.

## Clinical Relevance to Training
Disc herniation typically occurs posterolaterally where the annulus is thinnest. Exercises involving loaded spinal flexion (crunches, sit‑ups) increase intradiscal pressure and may exacerbate this risk. Anti‑extension and anti‑rotation exercises (plank, dead bug, Pallof press) build stability while protecting the discs.
