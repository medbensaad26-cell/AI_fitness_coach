# The Glenohumeral (Shoulder) Joint

## Structure
The glenohumeral joint is a ball‑and‑socket synovial joint formed by the articulation of the head of the humerus with the glenoid fossa of the scapula. The glenoid is shallow, providing a wide range of motion but limited bony stability. A fibrocartilaginous labrum deepens the socket slightly.

## Supporting Structures
- **Rotator cuff muscles**: supraspinatus, infraspinatus, teres minor, subscapularis – they form a musculotendinous cuff that dynamically stabilizes the joint.
- **Ligaments**: glenohumeral ligaments (superior, middle, inferior) reinforce the anterior capsule.
- **Bursae**: the subacromial bursa reduces friction between the acromion and the rotator cuff tendons.

## Movements & Muscle Actions
- **Flexion**: anterior deltoid, pectoralis major (clavicular head).
- **Abduction**: initiated by supraspinatus (0–15°), then deltoid (15–90°); beyond 90° requires upward rotation of the scapula.
- **External rotation**: infraspinatus, teres minor.
- **Internal rotation**: subscapularis.

## Clinical Relevance
Impingement occurs when the supraspinatus tendon and subacromial bursa are compressed between the humeral head and the acromion, often due to repetitive overhead activity or muscle imbalance. Maintaining a strong rotator cuff and avoiding exercises that combine abduction with internal rotation (e.g., upright rows, behind‑the‑neck presses) reduces risk.
