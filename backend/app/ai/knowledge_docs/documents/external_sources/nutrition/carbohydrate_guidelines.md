# Carbohydrate Guidelines for Athletic Performance

## Daily Needs Based on Activity Level
| Training Intensity/Volume | Carbohydrate Target |
|----------------------------|---------------------|
| Very light (skill, low‑intensity) | 3–5 g/kg body mass/day |
| Moderate (1 h/day) | 5–7 g/kg/day |
| High (1–3 h/day moderate‑high intensity) | 6–10 g/kg/day |
| Very high (4–5 h/day extreme exercise) | 8–12 g/kg/day |

## Pre‑Exercise Fueling
- 1–4 hours before: 1–4 g/kg of carbohydrates to top off glycogen stores.
- A pre‑event meal low in fat, fiber, and protein to speed gastric emptying.

## During Exercise
- For events >60 min: 30–60 g/h of carbohydrate (6–8% solution) can delay fatigue.
- For ultra‑endurance (>2.5 h): up to 90 g/h using multiple transportable carbohydrates (glucose + fructose) to increase oxidation rates.

## Post‑Exercise Glycogen Resynthesis
- Consume 1.0–1.2 g/kg/h of high‑glycemic carbohydrates within the first 4–6 hours after exhaustive exercise.
- Combining protein (0.2–0.4 g/kg) with carbohydrate may enhance glycogen storage when carbohydrate intake is suboptimal.
- Rapid refueling is critical for athletes with <24 h between sessions.

## Training Adaptation (“Train Low”)
Strategic training with low carbohydrate availability (“train low, compete high”) may enhance mitochondrial adaptations but should be periodized carefully to avoid compromising performance. Not recommended for youth or recreational athletes.

## Practical Recommendations
- Emphasize whole‑food sources: oats, brown rice, potatoes, fruits, whole‑grain bread.
- Use sports drinks, gels, or bars for convenience during prolonged or high‑intensity sessions.
- Individualize based on sport, goals, and gastrointestinal tolerance.
